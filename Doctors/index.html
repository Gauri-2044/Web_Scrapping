LATEST TECHNIQUES ONLY AVAILABLE AT ZENITH HOSPITAL

Rotablation

Rotaablation & good bed preparation is must to achieve good long term outcome.

  • Rotablation, also known as rotational atherectomy, is a medical procedure used to treat coronary artery disease (CAD). It involves the use of a specialized catheter with a tiny, diamond-coated burr at its tip.
  • During the procedure, the catheter is inserted into the affected artery through a small incision, usually in the groin. The catheter is carefully guided to the narrowed or blocked area of the coronary artery. The burr on the tip of the catheter is then activated and rotates at a high speed, around 150,000 to 200,000 revolutions per minute (RPM).
  • As the burr rotates, it grinds away the plaque or calcified deposits that have built up within the artery walls, essentially "scraping" them away. This process helps to restore blood flow by widening the artery and removing the blockage.
  • Rotablation is typically used in cases where the plaque or calcified deposits are too hard or resistant to other treatment methods, such as balloon angioplasty or stenting. It is often performed as a precursor to these procedures, preparing the artery for a successful stent placement.

IVUS

IVUS, or Intravascular Ultrasound, is a medical imaging technique used to visualize and assess the inside of blood vessels, particularly the coronary arteries. It provides detailed images of the artery walls and structures, helping doctors diagnose and treat various cardiovascular conditions.

During an IVUS procedure, a catheter with a tiny ultrasound probe at its tip is inserted into the blood vessel, typically through a small incision in the groin or wrist. The catheter is carefully advanced through the vessel until it reaches the area of interest, such as a narrowing or blockage.

Once in position, the ultrasound probe emits sound waves that bounce off the vessel walls. These sound waves create echoes, which are then captured by the probe and converted into detailed cross-sectional images of the artery. These images provide valuable information about the size, shape, and composition of the vessel, as well as the presence of any plaque buildup or abnormalities.

FFR

FFR, or Fractional Flow Reserve, is a medical test used to assess the severity of narrowing or blockage in the coronary arteries. It helps doctors determine if a blockage is significant enough to require further treatment, such as stenting or bypass surgery..

  • During an FFR procedure, a thin wire called a pressure wire is inserted into the coronary artery. The wire measures the blood pressure before and after the blockage. By comparing these pressure measurements, doctors can calculate the FFR value, which represents the ratio of blood flow beyond the blockage to the maximum potential blood flow in the absence of the blockage.
  • The FFR value is a number between 0 and 1. If the FFR value is below 0.80, it suggests that the blockage is causing a significant reduction in blood flow and may benefit from intervention. If the FFR value is above 0.80, it indicates that the blockage is not impeding blood flow significantly, and conservative management or medication may be sufficient.

4D Echo

A 4D echo, also known as a four-dimensional echocardiogram, is a medical imaging test that allows doctors to see detailed images of the heart in real-time. It is a non-invasive procedure that uses ultrasound technology to create moving images of the heart's structures and blood flow.

During a 4D echo, a small handheld device called a transducer is placed on the patient's chest. The transducer emits sound waves that bounce off the heart and create echoes. These echoes are then converted into images by a computer, which are displayed on a monitor.

Unlike a traditional 2D echo, which provides static images of the heart, a 4D echo adds the dimension of time. This means that doctors can see the heart beating and observe its movement and function in real-time. The 4D images can help identify any abnormalities or conditions affecting the heart, such as problems with the valves, chambers, or blood flow.

The procedure is typically painless and doesn't require any special preparation. It is commonly used to evaluate congenital heart defects, assess heart function, diagnose heart conditions, and monitor the progress of treatments. The information obtained from a 4D echo can help doctors make informed decisions about a patient's care and develop appropriate treatment plans.

Frequently Asked Questioins

Some question and answer related to heart problems.

  • What is heart attack?

    Heart attack occurs when blood supply to the heart is suddenly blocked, usually by a blood clot. It is also called Myocardial Infarction (MI) and is a medical emergency treated without delay.

  • What are symptoms of heart attack?

    Chest pain or discomfort at the center of the chest Pain in the left arm and shoulder, neck, jaw or back Sweating Feeling of weakness or dizziness Difficulty in breathing Rapid heartbeat Nausea or vomiting

  • What are risk factors of heart disease?

    According to American Heart Association, the major risk factors are: Age: More than 65 years Men have a higher risk than women Positive family history Cigarette smoking High blood pressure High cholesterol Inactive lifestyle Overweight Diabetes

  • What is coronary artery disease?

    When the blood vessels (called coronary arteries) which supply blood and oxygen to the heart are narrowed, it causes CAD, which can also cause a heart attack.

  • How to reduce high blooad pressure?

    Lead an active lifestyle, don't be a couch potato!Opt for a low salt diet, maintain an appropriate weight, and avoid alcohol intake and smoking

  • What is coronary angiography?

    Angiography is a specialized X-ray study of the blood vessels of the heart. It is a gold standard diagnostic test to detect blockage or narrowing of the heart blood vessels.
